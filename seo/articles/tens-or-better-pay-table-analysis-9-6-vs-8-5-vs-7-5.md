# Tens or Better Pay Table Analysis: 9/6 vs. 8/5 vs. 7/5

In the world of video poker, the pay table is the only "map" you need to navigate the waters of profitability. Tens or Better is a unique game because it pays for a pair of 10s, which increases hit frequency but often leads casinos to offer lower payouts for the "middle" hands. In this analysis, we will compare the most common pay tables and examine the **EV Differential** that affects your long-term success. Finding the **best odds for tens or better** is a matter of simple arithmetic.

## What is Pay Table Verification?

**Pay Table Verification** is the process of looking at the payouts for a Full House and a Flush (the 4th and 5th hands from the top) to determine the overall Return to Player (RTP). In Tens or Better, these numbers are the primary levers the casino uses to adjust the house edge.

## Comparing the Tables

### 1. The "Full Pay" 9/6 Tens or Better
*   **Full House:** 9 coins
*   **Flush:** 6 coins
*   **Theoretical RTP:** 99.14%
*   **Volatility Index:** Low

This is the holy grail for Tens or Better players. It offers a very fair game with a house edge of less than 1%. It is ideal for players who want to grind out a long session on a modest bankroll.

### 2. The "Short Pay" 8/5 Tens or Better
*   **Full House:** 8 coins
*   **Flush:** 5 coins
*   **Theoretical RTP:** 96.26%
*   **Volatility Index:** Moderate

The drop from 9/6 to 8/5 is catastrophic for the player. You are losing nearly 3% in expected value. In a session of 1,000 hands at $5 per hand, you would expect to lose $144 more on an 8/5 machine than on a 9/6 machine.

### 3. The "Low Pay" 7/5 Tens or Better
*   **Full House:** 7 coins
*   **Flush:** 5 coins
*   **Theoretical RTP:** 95.21%
*   **Volatility Index:** Moderate

At this level, Tens or Better becomes worse than many high-denomination slot machines. The house edge of nearly 5% makes it almost impossible to have a winning session over the long term.

## The Impact of the EV Differential

The **EV Differential** is the gap between the return of a perfect play on a good machine vs. a bad one. In Tens or Better, the gap between 9/6 and 7/5 is nearly 4%. This difference is the reason why some players walk away winners and others consistently lose.
