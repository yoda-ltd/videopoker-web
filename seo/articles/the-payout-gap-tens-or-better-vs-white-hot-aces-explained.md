# The Payout Gap: Tens or Better vs. White Hot Aces Explained

The "Payout Gap" between Tens or Better and White Hot Aces (WHA) represents the ultimate choice in video poker: "Safety" versus "Strike." This **best video poker strategy comparison** breaks down how **Payout Compression** and **Variance Scaling** create two radically different gambling experiences from the same basic deck of cards.

## The Mathematical Foundation: Threshold vs. Jackpot

**Tens or Better** is designed for maximum "Time on Device." By paying you "Money Back" for a pair of Tens, the game provides the most frequent winning threshold in the casino. To fund this "Safety," the game uses **Payout Compression** on "Flush" and "Full House" hands. The **video poker house edge analysis** is based on "Volume and Stability."

**White Hot Aces** is designed for the "Strike." It is a Jacks or Better variant that pays a massive "White Hot" premium for 4 Aces. To fund this, the game uses heavy **Payout Compression** on Two-Pair (which only pays 1-to-1). This creates a massive **EV Gap** in bankroll stability. You are "Bleeding" bankroll on every Two-Pair hand you hit, hoping for the 400-to-1 or 800-to-1 Ace hit.

## Variance Scaling and the Bankroll Buffer

1.  **Tens or Better:** The variance is low (around 18-20). It is a "Low-Risk" game. The **Variance Scaling** is very shallow; your bankroll moves slowly and predictably. You can play for hours on a small budget.
2.  **White Hot Aces:** The variance is high (often 40+). It is a "High-Risk" game. The **Variance Scaling** is steep. Because you lose your bet on every Two-Pair hand, your bankroll will "Liquidate" rapidly if you don't hit a "Big" hand. This requires a large bankroll buffer.

## Strategy Bifurcation: The "Hold" Factor

The **Strategy Bifurcation** is a total reversal of player intent:
*   In **Tens or Better**, you are "Survival Hunting." You hold high cards (Tens and up) with extreme priority to get your money back. Your goal is simply to "Stay in the Game."
*   In **White Hot Aces**, you are "Ace Hunting." You will hold a single Ace with extreme priority. You treat the Ace as a "Jackpot" trigger. You accept the "Loss" on Two-Pair as a necessary evil.

For the beginner or the "Safe" player, Tens or Better is the clear winner. For the "Jackpot Hunter" who wants the "Rush" of the Aces, White Hot Aces offers the ultimate "Strike" potential. Both require a disciplined approach and an understanding of the "Cost" of the safety (or the strike).
