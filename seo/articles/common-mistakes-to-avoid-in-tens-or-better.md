# Common Mistakes to Avoid in Tens or Better: Surviving the "Low-Pay" Grind

Tens or Better is often viewed as the "Entry Level" game for video poker. Because the minimum winning hand is lower (a pair of 10s instead of Jacks), the game feels easier. However, for a professional "grinder," this "easiness" is a trap. The lower winning threshold is always balanced by lower payouts elsewhere in the table.

If you are learning **how to become a professional video poker player**, Tens or Better is a lesson in "Efficiency." Here are the common strategy errors and the **expert video poker strategy tips** needed to maintain **EV Maximization** in this low-volatility environment.

## 1. The "Safety" Illusion
Because you win more often in Tens or Better, players tend to play more "loosely."

*   **The Error:** Holding a pair of 10s and feeling "safe" while ignoring a more valuable 3-card Royal Flush draw.
*   **The Pro Fix:** A professional doesn't care about "winning more often"; they care about the "Expected Value" (EV). While the pair of 10s guarantees your money back, the 3-card Royal Flush draw has a higher long-term value. Amateurs take the "sure thing," but a pro takes the "math thing."

## 2. Misplaying the "High Card" Logic
In Jacks or Better, you have four "high cards" (J, Q, K, A). In Tens or Better, you have five.

*   **The Error:** Applying Jacks or Better "high card" hierarchy to Tens or Better.
*   **The Pro Fix:** The 10 is now a "power card." This changes the math for Straights and "High Card" draws. A professional **Penalty Card Awareness** includes the 10 as a card that can both complete a win and "penalize" other draws. If you don't treat the 10 with the same respect as a Jack, you are leaking EV on every hand.

## 3. Ignoring the "Short-Pay" Table
Because Tens or Better is often marketed to recreational players, casinos frequently "hide" poor pay tables on these machines.

*   **The Error:** Playing a 6/5 or 5/4 Tens or Better machine.
*   **The Pro Fix:** A professional never plays a machine where the Full House and Flush payouts have been "gutted" to pay for the Tens-or-Better minimum. You must look for "Full Pay" versions. If the machine doesn't offer at least a 99% return, a pro walks away. There is no such thing as a "good deal" on a bad pay table.

## 4. Neglecting "Session Longevity"
Because Tens or Better is low-variance, it is a "Slow Burn" game.

*   **The Error:** Losing focus because the game isn't "exciting" enough.
*   **The Pro Fix:** Success in Tens or Better comes from massive volume. You aren't looking for a "Big Score"; you are looking for 1,000 hands of perfect play per hour. A professional uses this game to "churn" for casino points or to meet bonus requirements. If you get bored and start making "creative" plays, you've already lost.

## 5. Misjudging the "Two-Pair" Value
In many Tens or Better games, the Two-Pair payout is the key to your survival.

*   **The Error:** Breaking a low Two-Pair to draw for a Three-of-a-Kind.
*   **The Pro Fix:** Never break a winning Two-Pair. The 2:1 payout is what sustains your **bankroll fluctuations** and keeps you in the game. Professionals protect their "bankroll churn" by taking every 2:1 win they are offered.

## The Tens or Better "Professional" Checklist
To play Tens or Better like a pro, you must be a master of the "mundane."

*   **The Professional Workflow**:
    1.  **Treat the 10 as a Jack**: It is a high card now. Act accordingly.
    2.  **Verify the Pay Table**: Look for 6/5 or higher on the Full House/Flush.
    3.  **Volume is King**: Play fast, play perfect, and collect your comps.
    4.  **No Emotional Plays**: A pair of 10s is a tool, not a victory.

By mastering the subtle shift in the high-card hierarchy and focusing on mathematical efficiency, you can turn the "easiest" game in the casino into a reliable part of your professional repertoire.
