# Aces and Eights Video Poker: Pay Table Analysis (9/6 vs. 8/5 vs. 7/5)

Aces and Eights is a variation of video poker that rewards players for hitting specific four-of-a-kind hands, namely Aces, Eights, and Sevens. While these specialized payouts make the game exciting, the overall Return to Player (RTP) is heavily dictated by the payouts for the Full House and the Flush.

In this analysis, we will compare the common pay tables for Aces and Eights: **9/6**, **8/5**, and **7/5**, and see how they impact your long-term returns and strategy.

## Understanding the "Full Pay" Table (9/6)

The "9/6" label refers to the payout for a **Full House (9 coins)** and a **Flush (6 coins)** per coin bet. In the world of video poker, a 9/6 Aces and Eights machine is considered "Full Pay" and is highly sought after by savvy players.

### 9/6 Pay Table (at 1-5 coins)
*   **Royal Flush:** 250 (800 for 5 coins)
*   **Straight Flush:** 50
*   **4 Aces or 8s:** 80
*   **4 7s:** 50
*   **Other 4 of a Kind:** 25
*   **Full House:** 9
*   **Flush:** 6
*   **Straight:** 4
*   **3 of a Kind:** 3
*   **Two Pair:** 2
*   **Jacks or Better:** 1

**9/6 Expected Return (RTP): 99.78%**

With a return of 99.78%, a 9/6 machine is one of the best games in any casino, provided you play with perfect strategy.

## The Mid-Tier: 8/5 Pay Table

Casinos often reduce the payouts for the Full House and Flush to increase their house edge. In an 8/5 machine, the Full House payout drops to 8 coins, and the Flush drops to 5 coins.

### Impact of the 8/5 Drop
*   **Full House:** 8 (down from 9)
*   **Flush:** 5 (down from 6)

**8/5 Expected Return (RTP): 98.63%**

While a 98.63% return is still better than most slot machines, you are losing more than **1.1% of your total return** compared to the 9/6 version. Over thousands of hands, this adds up to significant losses.

## The Low-Tier: 7/5 Pay Table

The 7/5 version is common in airports, bars, and high-traffic tourist areas where players are less likely to notice the difference. This version drops the Full House payout even further to 7 coins.

### Impact of the 7/5 Drop
*   **Full House:** 7 (down from 8)
*   **Flush:** 5 (stays at 5)

**7/5 Expected Return (RTP): 97.52%**

At 97.52%, the house edge is now nearly **2.5%**. This version of Aces and Eights should generally be avoided if a better pay table is available nearby.

### Why the Flush and Full House Matter Most

You might wonder why a small 1-coin difference in the Full House or Flush matters so much when the game has 80-coin payouts for Four Aces. The reason is **frequency**.

*   A Full House occurs roughly once every **85 hands**.
*   A Flush occurs roughly once every **90 hands**.
*   Four Aces occur only once every **5,000+ hands**.

Because you hit Full Houses and Flushes so much more often, even a 1-coin reduction has a massive cumulative effect on your bankroll.

## Strategy Adjustments

While the basic strategy for Aces and Eights remains relatively similar across these pay tables, your **expected win rate** changes drastically. On a 7/5 machine, you will find your bankroll depleting much faster, meaning you need a larger bankroll to wait for those high-paying four-of-a-kind hands.

## Conclusion: Always Hunt for the 9/6

When playing Aces and Eights, the first thing you should do is check the pay table. Look for the 9 for the Full House and the 6 for the Flush. If you see 8/5 or 7/5, know that you are paying a much higher "tax" to the casino. In the long run, finding a 9/6 machine is the single most important strategy for winning at Aces and Eights.

Always play 5 coins to unlock the 800-to-1 bonus on the Royal Flush, as this is essential to achieving the listed RTP percentages.
