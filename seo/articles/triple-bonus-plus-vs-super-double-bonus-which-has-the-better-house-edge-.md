# Triple Bonus Plus vs. Super Double Bonus: Which Has the Better House Edge?

When comparing high-volatility video poker machines, two names often stand at the top: **Triple Bonus Plus** and **Super Double Bonus**. Both games reward specific four-of-a-kind (Quads) combinations, making them exciting choices for players looking for a big score. But which one offers a better long-term **Expected Value (EV)**, and how do they differ in strategy?

## The Payout Breakdown

Both games are variations of Jacks or Better, but they differ significantly in how they pay for Quads.

### Triple Bonus Plus (9/6 Pay Table)
*   **4 Aces:** 800 coins (on a 5-coin bet)
*   **4 2s, 3s, or 4s:** 400 coins
*   **4 5s through Kings:** 250 coins
*   **Full House:** 9 coins
*   **Flush:** 6 coins

### Super Double Bonus (9/5 Pay Table)
*   **4 Aces:** 800 coins
*   **4 2s, 3s, or 4s:** 400 coins
*   **4 Kings, Queens, or Jacks:** 400 coins
*   **4 5s through 10s:** 250 coins
*   **Full House:** 9 coins
*   **Flush:** 5 coins

## House Edge and Return to Player (RTP)

The house edge is the long-term mathematical advantage the casino has over the player. In video poker, we often talk in terms of RTP (Return to Player).

*   **9/6 Triple Bonus Plus:** Offers an RTP of approximately **99.58%**. This is a powerhouse for skilled players.
*   **9/5 Super Double Bonus:** Offers an RTP of approximately **99.69%**.

**The Verdict:** By a razor-thin margin, **Super Double Bonus** (in its full-pay version) generally offers a better long-term EV. However, these "full-pay" tables are becoming increasingly rare. You must always check the specific pay table on the machine. An 8/5 Triple Bonus Plus (97.67% RTP) is far worse than a 9/5 Super Double Bonus.

## Variance: The High Volatility Factor

Both games are considered **high volatility video poker strategy** tests. Because so much of the RTP is tied up in rare four-of-a-kind hands, you will experience longer losing streaks compared to standard Jacks or Better.

*   **Triple Bonus Plus:** Focuses its highest bonuses on Aces and low cards (2, 3, 4).
*   **Super Double Bonus:** Extends those 400-coin bonuses to include Jacks, Queens, and Kings. This makes the distribution of big wins slightly more frequent, though the reduced Flush payout (5 vs. 6) compensates for this.

## Strategy Comparison: How to Hold

When playing either of these games, using a **triple bonus poker trainer** or a strategy calculator is essential.

1.  **Breaking a Full House:** In both games, if you are dealt a Full House that includes three Aces, you should actually *break* the Full House and hold only the three Aces. The potential 800-coin payout for the fourth Ace is worth more in EV than the guaranteed 9-coin Full House.
2.  **High Card Priority:** In Super Double Bonus, high cards (J, Q, K) become much more valuable because they can now pay 400 coins for a Quad. In Triple Bonus Plus, these are "standard" Quads (250 coins), so you don't prioritize them as aggressively as you would Aces.
3.  **The Kicker Rule:** Unlike Double Double Bonus, neither of these games uses "kickers" (a specific fifth card) to boost the payout of a Quad. This simplifies the strategy significantly.

## Conclusion

If you can find a full-pay 9/5 Super Double Bonus machine, it's technically the superior choice. However, 9/6 Triple Bonus Plus is nearly as good and often easier to find in many casinos. Both games demand a disciplined approach and a large enough bankroll to survive the high-variance nature of the bonus payouts. Whichever you choose, ensure you've mastered the specific hold patterns for that game's pay table to minimize the house edge.
