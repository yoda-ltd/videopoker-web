## A Brief History of Tens or Better

Tens or Better emerged in the early days of electronic video poker as a way to increase the frequency of winning hands. By lowering the threshold for a "payout-eligible" pair from Jacks to Tens, manufacturers created a game with a higher "hit frequency." This made it particularly appealing to recreational players who preferred more frequent, albeit smaller, wins to keep their bankroll moving. While it never reached the same level of ubiquity as Jacks or Better in modern Vegas casinos, it remains a staple in multi-game machines and online platforms.

## Pay Table Verification: 9/6 vs. 8/5

In video poker, your most important skill is **Pay Table Verification**. The difference between a winning session and a losing one often comes down to the payout for the Full House and Flush.

*   **9/6 Tens or Better:** This version pays 9 coins for a Full House and 6 coins for a Flush (per coin bet). This is considered the "Full Pay" version.
*   **8/5 Tens or Better:** This version reduces those payouts to 8 and 5 respectively.

The **EV Differential** (Expected Value) between these two is significant. A 9/6 machine typically offers an RTP (Return to Player) of approximately 99.14% with perfect strategy, whereas the 8/5 version drops to around 96.26%. Always hunt for the **best odds for tens or better** by checking the middle of the pay table.

## Tens or Better vs. Jacks or Better: Comparative Analysis

The primary difference is the volatility. Because you get paid for a pair of Tens, the hit frequency in Tens or Better is higher than in Jacks or Better. However, to compensate for this, the payouts for higher-ranking hands are often slightly adjusted, or the overall RTP is slightly lower than a Full Pay Jacks or Better (9/6) machine (which hits 99.54%).

Tens or Better has a lower **Volatility Index** than Jacks or Better. It is the "smoothest" game in the non-wild card category, making it ideal for players who want to maximize their time at the machine without experiencing the rapid bankroll swings common in Bonus Poker variants.
