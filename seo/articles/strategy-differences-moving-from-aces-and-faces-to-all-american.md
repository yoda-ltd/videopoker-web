# Strategy Differences: Moving from Aces and Faces to All American

If you are a regular **Aces and Faces** player, your strategy is built around maximizing your chances of hitting Four of a Kinds with high cards. Transitioning to **All American** requires a fundamental shift in your priorities. You are moving from a "High-Card" strategy to a "Sequential/Suited" strategy.

Here are the key differences you need to master to make the switch successfully.

## 1. The 8:1 Payout Revolution

The most important difference is how much the game pays for Straights and Flushes.
*   **Aces and Faces:** Straights usually pay 4:1 or 5:1, and Flushes pay 5:1 or 6:1.
*   **All American:** Both Straights and Flushes typically pay **8:1**.

**The Strategy Shift:** In Aces and Faces, you almost never break a pair of any kind to chase a straight or flush. In All American, you will **regularly break a "Low Pair"** (2s through 10s) if you have 4 cards to a Straight or a Flush. The 8:1 reward is simply too high to ignore.

## 2. The Devaluation of Two Pair

*   **Aces and Faces:** Two Pair pays **2:1**. It is a reliable way to grow your bankroll.
*   **All American:** Two Pair pays **1:1**. It is a "push."

**The Strategy Shift:** In Aces and Faces, you are thrilled to get Two Pair. In All American, you shouldn't get too attached to it. While you still hold it if you have it, you should be much more aggressive in chasing "Royal Flush" draws, even if it means breaking a Two Pair.

## 3. The Straight Flush "Hunt"

*   **Aces and Faces:** Straight Flush pays 50:1.
*   **All American:** Straight Flush pays **200:1**.

**The Strategy Shift:** In Aces and Faces, a 3-card Straight Flush draw is a "backup plan." In All American, it is a primary goal. You should prioritize 3-card Straight Flush draws (with no gaps) over holding single high cards or even sometimes over a low pair.

## 4. High-Card Importance

In Aces and Faces, you prioritize Jacks, Queens, Kings, and Aces because they offer bonus quad payouts.
*   **The Difference:** In All American, an Ace doesn't offer a special quad bonus. Its only special value is that it pays "Jacks or Better" (1:1).
*   **The Strategy Shift:** Stop "over-holding" high cards. If you have a Jack and four cards to a Straight, keep the Straight draw. The "Faces" aren't as special in All American as they are in the game you’re used to.

## 5. Bankroll and Variance

*   **Aces and Faces:** Low Variance (~19.8). A very steady, "safe" game.
*   **All American:** High Variance (~30.0). A "wilder" ride.

**The Transition Tip:** Don't let a losing streak in All American discourage you. Because of the 1:1 Two Pair payout, you will lose your credits faster on a "standard" run. You are waiting for the "burst" payouts (8:1, 34:1, 200:1) to put you back in the black.

## Summary Checklist for the Switch

1.  **Check the Flush/Straight Payout:** If it’s 8:1, you are in All American territory.
2.  **Break Low Pairs:** If you have 4 cards to a Flush or open-ended Straight.
3.  **Prioritize 3-Card Straight Flushes:** Over single "Face" cards.
4.  **Accept the "Even Money" Two Pair:** Don't get frustrated when you don't double your money on Two Pair.

## Conclusion

Moving from Aces and Faces to All American is like moving from a "Safe" investment to a "Growth" stock. You are taking more risks on draws in exchange for much larger payouts on the mid-tier hands. If you can master the 8:1 math and stay disciplined during the variance, you'll find All American to be one of the most rewarding games in the casino.
