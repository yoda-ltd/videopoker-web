# Why Dice Poker is Better Than Keno Poker for Beginners

The **history of Dice Poker** and the **history of Keno Poker** are both rooted in the early 2010s, but they cater to very different types of players. For a beginner looking to understand the **evolution of video poker games**, Dice Poker is almost always the better entry point.

## Simplicity vs. Complexity: The Bonus Game vs. The Number-Based Logic

In **Dice Poker**, the game is designed to be a "standard" video poker experience with a dice-based bonus game. To play the full game, you must bet 5 credits per hand. The **Action Gaming patents** governing Dice Poker require the player to "roll" the dice after a winning hand to determine a multiplier. This is a standard in the **Casino Adoption Curve** for "Plus-One" games.

In **Keno Poker**, the strategy is significantly more difficult. To play the full game, you must mark numbers on your poker cards as if they were keno spots. This is a steep **Casino Adoption Curve** for a beginner.

## Key Reasons to Choose Dice Poker First

1.  **Variety Over Volatility:** Dice Poker offers multiple games (Keno, Blackjack, Video Poker) on one machine. A beginner can experiment with different variants like **Jacks or Better** or Deuces Wild without the high-stakes "keno spots" of Keno Poker.
2.  **Standard Strategy:** The strategy for Dice Poker is identical to standard video poker. In Keno Poker, your strategy *must* change. For example, in **Keno Poker**, the "hold" is often secondary, as the value of the keno "hits" often outweighs the value of the poker hand itself.
3.  **Bankroll Preservation:** Because Dice Poker allows for a 5-credit bet, a beginner's bankroll will last longer than it would on a Keno Poker machine. The **history of Keno Poker** shows it was designed for high-limit keno lounges because it offered a faster way to play keno with a poker "twist."

## Conclusion

While both games are icons in the **evolution of video poker games**, Dice Poker's "standard" approach makes it the friendliest choice for those just starting out. Save the "keno spots" for when you've mastered the basic math of a single-hand draw.
