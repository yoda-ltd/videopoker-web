# Double Down Stud Pay Table Analysis: 9/6 vs 8/5 vs 7/5

Double Down Stud is a unique video poker variant where you don't discard cards. Instead, you see four cards and decide whether to double your bet before seeing the fifth. Because there is no "draw," the pay tables for Double Down Stud look very different from standard Jacks or Better.

In this analysis, we look at the most common Double Down Stud pay tables and explain which ones offer the best value for the player.

## Understanding the "Double Down" Pay Table

In Double Down Stud, the game typically pays for a **Pair of 6s or Better**. This is lower than the Jacks or Better requirement because you have no chance to improve your hand through a draw.

The "Full Pay" version of Double Down Stud is often referred to as a **9/6** table, but the meaning of those numbers is slightly different here.

### The Key Numbers: Full House and Flush
Just like other games, the Full House and Flush payouts are the "sliders" casinos use to adjust the house edge.

*   **9/6:** Full House (9), Flush (6) - RTP: ~98.5%
*   **8/5:** Full House (8), Flush (5) - RTP: ~96.5%
*   **7/5:** Full House (7), Flush (5) - RTP: ~95.0%

## Return to Player (RTP) Comparison

| Version | Full House | Flush | Theoretical RTP | House Edge |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Full Pay (9/6)** | 9 | 6 | **98.49%** | 1.51% |
| **Standard (8/5)** | 8 | 5 | **96.44%** | 3.56% |
| **Low Pay (7/5)** | 7 | 5 | **94.98%** | 5.02% |

### Why is the RTP Lower than Jacks or Better?
You'll notice that even the "Full Pay" 98.49% is lower than the 99.54% found in Jacks or Better. This is because Double Down Stud is a **betting game**, not a **drawing game**. 

The strategy in Double Down Stud isn't about which cards to hold (you hold all of them), it's about **when to double your bet**. A skilled player who knows exactly when to double down can achieve the 98.49% return, while a casual player will likely return closer to 95%.

## The "Winning Hand" Requirement

Most Double Down Stud machines use the following base payout table (per coin):
*   **Royal Flush:** 800
*   **Straight Flush:** 200
*   **Four of a Kind:** 50
*   **Full House:** 8 or 9
*   **Flush:** 5 or 6
*   **Straight:** 4
*   **Three of a Kind:** 3
*   **Two Pair:** 2
*   **Pair (6s to Aces):** 1

**Crucial Note:** If you have anything less than a pair of 6s (like a pair of 2s), the hand pays **zero**. This is why the "Double Down" decision is so critical. You only want to double your bet when you have a high probability of ending with at least a pair of 6s.

## Strategy: Doubling Down Based on the Pay Table

The pay table influences when you should be aggressive.
*   **On a 9/6 Machine:** You should be more willing to double down on "inside" straight flushes or high-card flushes because the payout for hitting them is higher.
*   **On an 8/5 or 7/5 Machine:** You should be more conservative. Only double down when you already have a "Pat Hand" (a winning pair or better) to protect your bankroll from the higher house edge.

## Summary Checklist

Before playing Double Down Stud, check these three things:
1.  **Pair Requirement:** Does it pay for a pair of 6s? Some "bad" machines only pay for 8s or 10s.
2.  **Full House/Flush:** Is it 9/6? If it's 8/5, you are losing 2% in return.
3.  **Four of a Kind:** Does it pay 50? Some variants pay only 25, which destroys the game's value.

**Verdict:** Double Down Stud is a fun change of pace, but mathematically, **it is rarely better than a standard Jacks or Better or Double Bonus machine.** Only play if you find a 9/6 machine and enjoy the unique betting mechanic. Avoid 7/5 machines at all costs, as the 5% house edge is comparable to slot machines.
