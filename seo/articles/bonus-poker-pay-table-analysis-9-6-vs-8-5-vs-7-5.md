# Bonus Poker Pay Table Analysis: 9/6 vs. 8/5 vs. 7/5

Bonus Poker is a popular variant of Jacks or Better that offers higher payouts for specific Four of a Kind hands. While Jacks or Better only pays 25-for-1 for any Quad, **Bonus Poker pays 80-for-1 for four Aces and 40-for-1 for four 2s, 3s, or 4s**. 

To offset this increase, the game often reduces the payout for common hands like the Full House or Flush. In this article, we'll break down the math for the three most common pay table versions: 9/6, 8/5, and 7/5.

## The Pay Table Structures

Each version is defined by the payout for a **Full House** and a **Flush**.

| Hand | 9/6 (Full Pay) | 8/5 (Reduced) | 7/5 (Low Pay) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Royal Flush (5-coin)** | **4,000** | **4,000** | **4,000** |
| Straight Flush | 250 | 250 | 250 |
| **4 Aces** | **400** | **400** | **400** |
| **4 2s, 3s, 4s** | **200** | **200** | **200** |
| 4 5s through Kings | 125 | 125 | 125 |
| Full House | 45 | 40 | 35 |
| Flush | 30 | 25 | 25 |
| Straight | 20 | 20 | 20 |
| Three of a Kind | 15 | 15 | 15 |
| Two Pair | 10 | 10 | 10 |
| Jacks or Better | 5 | 5 | 5 |

## Expected Returns (RTP)

The differences in the Full House and Flush payouts might seem small, but they have a massive impact on the house edge and your long-term bankroll.

### 1. The 9/6 Version (Full Pay)
*   **Return to Player (RTP):** 99.64%
*   **House Edge:** 0.36%
*   **Strategy:** This is the "gold standard" of Bonus Poker. If you can find this machine, you are playing one of the best games in the casino. With a decent players club card, you can often reach a positive expected value (over 100% return).

### 2. The 8/5 Version
*   **Return to Player (RTP):** 99.17%
*   **House Edge:** 0.83%
*   **Impact:** By dropping the Full House from 9 to 8 and the Flush from 6 to 5, the casino has increased its profit margin by nearly 0.5%. While this is still a playable game compared to most slot machines, it is significantly worse than the 9/6 version.

### 3. The 7/5 Version
*   **Return to Player (RTP):** 98.01%
*   **House Edge:** 1.99%
*   **Impact:** This is a "tight" machine often found in bars, airports, or tourist-heavy casinos. While you still get the high-Quad excitement, the lower return on common hands will drain your bankroll much faster.

## Why Pay Tables Matter

Because you see a Full House or a Flush much more often than a Four of a Kind or a Royal Flush, those small differences in the 9/6 vs. 8/5 payouts add up quickly. 

- In a **9/6** game, you receive **9 coins** for every 1-coin bet on a Full House. 
- In a **7/5** game, you receive only **7 coins**. 

Over a session of 1,000 hands, where you might hit 10-15 Full Houses, that's a difference of 30-45 coins—or roughly 6-9 max-bet hands.

## Strategy Adjustments

The strategy for Bonus Poker is remarkably similar to Jacks or Better, with one key exception: **You value Aces more highly.** Because four Aces pay 80-for-1, you are slightly more inclined to hold an Ace than a single face card compared to Jacks or Better.

However, the strategy does *not* change significantly between the 9/6, 8/5, and 7/5 versions. You should always use the optimal 9/6 strategy, as it provides the best expected value regardless of the machine's specific payout.

## Summary

Always check the pay table before you sit down. Look at the payout for the **Full House** and the **Flush**.

- **9/6:** Play all day!
- **8/5:** Acceptable if no better games are available.
- **7/5:** Avoid if possible; the house edge is too high.

By choosing the right pay table, you significantly increase your chances of a winning session and prolong your play time. Always bet the **max 5 coins** for the Royal Flush bonus.
