# Understanding Volatility and Variance in Video Poker: Multi-Hand Scaling and Combinatorial Convergence

In the world of professional gambling, the terms "volatility" and "variance" are the foundations of bankroll survival. In multi-hand environments (3, 5, 10, 50, and 100-play), these concepts evolve into **Multi-Hand Volatility Scaling** and **Combinatorial Convergence**. This guide explores the mathematics of **Shared-Base Variance** and how it determines the **best video poker for high volume** play.

## Defining the Terms: Variance vs. Volatility Scaling

In video poker, **variance** measures the dispersion of results around the **Expected Value (EV)**. In multi-hand play, this dispersion is affected by two competing forces:

1.  **Shared-Base Variance:** Since all hands share the same initial deal, the outcomes are correlated. This "front-loads" the variance onto the single event of the deal.
2.  **Independent Draw Randomness:** Since the draws for each hand are independent, the "Replacement Phase" provides a **Smoothing Effect**, where the variance per hand decreases as the number of hands increases.

### Multi-Hand Volatility Scaling
A common mistake is assuming that 100-play is 100 times more volatile than 1-play. Mathematically, the **Standard Deviation** of a 100-play deal is roughly **5 to 6 times higher** than a single-play deal (not 100 times), because the independent draws partially offset the shared deal. However, the *required bankroll* scales significantly to handle the "Shared-Base" swings.

## Combinatorial Convergence and the "Long Run"

**Combinatorial Convergence** is the speed at which your actual return moves closer to the theoretical **Expected Return (ER)**.

- **The Smoothing Effect:** In 100-play, you are running 100 **Independent Trials** per deal. For common hands like Three-of-a-Kind, you will see statistical convergence much faster than a single-line player.
- **The "Century" Grind:** A professional can play 50,000 hands in a single afternoon using 100-play machines. This high volume "compresses" the time required to reach the long run, but it requires a bankroll capable of sustaining **Multi-Hand Volatility Scaling**.

## Variance Across Different Games: Jacks or Better vs. Triple Double Bonus

When choosing the **best video poker for high volume**, the game's intrinsic variance determines your "Burn Rate."

### 100-Play Jacks or Better (Low Variance)
- **Variance Index:** ~19.5 (Single).
- **Behavior:** The frequency of small wins (Jacks or Better, Two Pair) creates a strong "Smoothing Effect" via the independent draws. Even with a mediocre base hand, the 100 draws often "claw back" a significant portion of the bet.

### 100-Play Triple Double Bonus (Extreme Variance)
- **Variance Index:** ~100+ (Single).
- **Behavior:** TDB is dominated by **Shared-Base Variance**. Because the RTP is so top-heavy (Four-of-a-Kind with a Kicker), the independent draws on a "nothing" base hand rarely provide enough of a "Smoothing Effect" to matter. This leads to rapid bankroll depletion during "deal droughts."

## The Math of the "Shared Base Hand"

The **Shared Base Hand** is the most significant factor in multi-hand volatility.
- **Positive Correlation:** Holding 4-to-a-Royal in 100-play gives you a massive "expected win" because you have 100 independent shots at a 4,000-coin payout.
- **Negative Correlation:** Being dealt a "nothing" hand (e.g., 2, 5, 8, 10, Q) across 100 lines means you are paying for 100 low-probability draws simultaneously. This is the "cost of volume."

## Bankroll Management: Solving for Multi-Hand Volatility

To survive the **Multi-Hand Volatility Scaling**, professionals use the following benchmarks for their **multi hand video poker strategy**:

1.  **Bankroll Depth:** For 100-play, your total bankroll should be at least **3 to 4 times larger** than what you would need for the same total "coin-in" on a single-line machine.
2.  **Risk of Ruin (RoR):** Use a **Combinatorial Convergence** calculator to ensure your RoR remains below 1%.
3.  **Game Selection:** If your bankroll is limited, choose **best video poker for high volume** games with a lower Variance Index (like JoB or Bonus Poker) to maximize the "Smoothing Effect" of the independent draws.

## Conclusion

Mastering volatility in multi-hand video poker is about balancing the power of the **Shared Base Hand** with the reliability of **Independent Draw Randomness**. By understanding **Multi-Hand Volatility Scaling** and choosing games that offer the right level of **Combinatorial Convergence**, you can leverage high volume to reach your **Expected Return (ER)** faster and more safely than ever before.
