# Royal Aces Pay Table Analysis: 9/6 vs 8/5 vs 7/5
Category: Variant Guide

In the landscape of high-volatility video poker, **Royal Aces** stands out as a variant that demands respect for its aggressive pay table structure. Unlike standard Jacks or Better or even traditional Bonus Poker, Royal Aces shifts the weight of the Expected Value (EV) significantly toward the top end of the spectrum—specifically targeting the "Aces-heavy" hands. This article provides a comprehensive **royal aces video poker strategy** overview by analyzing the math behind the 9/6, 8/5, and 7/5 pay tables.

## The 2000-Coin Bonus: A Game Changer

The defining feature of Royal Aces is the massive payout for Quad Aces. While standard Bonus Poker might pay 400 coins (on a 5-coin bet) for four Aces, Royal Aces elevates this to a staggering **2,000 coins**. This is exactly half of a Royal Flush payout, but it occurs much more frequently.

### Quad Aces: Royal Aces vs. Bonus Poker
| Hand | Royal Aces (5-Coin) | Standard Bonus Poker |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Four Aces | 2,000 | 400 |
| Four 2s-4s | 400 | 200 |
| Four 5s-Ks | 250 | 125 |

This 2,000-coin "mini-jackpot" fundamentally alters your **Aces-heavy EV**. In Royal Aces, the value of holding a single Ace or a pair of Aces is significantly higher than in other variants because the reward for "getting there" (hitting the quads) is so immense.

## Pay Table Breakdown: 9/6 vs. 8/5 vs. 7/5

The numbers "9/6" or "8/5" typically refer to the payouts for the Full House and Flush, respectively. Because so much of the return is tied up in the Quad Aces bonus, the "small" hands are where the casino takes its cut.

### 9/6 Royal Aces (The "Gold Standard")
*   **Full House:** 9x
*   **Flush:** 6x
*   **Return to Player (RTP):** ~99.2% (with perfect strategy)
*   **Variance:** Extremely High
*   **Analysis:** This is the version you want. The 6-coin Flush payout provides enough "oxygen" to your bankroll to survive the dry spells between hitting big Quads.

### 8/5 Royal Aces
*   **Full House:** 8x
*   **Flush:** 5x
*   **Return to Player (RTP):** ~97.4%
*   **Analysis:** This is a common "strip" version. The loss of 1 coin on both the Full House and Flush represents a nearly 2% drop in total return. This makes your "kicker dependencies" even more critical—you cannot afford to misplay marginal hands when the base return is this low.

### 7/5 Royal Aces
*   **Full House:** 7x
*   **Flush:** 5x
*   **Return to Player (RTP):** ~96.3%
*   **Analysis:** Avoid if possible. The pay table variance remains high because the 2,000-coin Quad Ace is still there, but the "grind" hands (Full House/Flush) are so weak that your bankroll will likely deplete before you hit the jackpot.

## Strategic Implications: Kicker Dependencies and Aces-Heavy EV

When playing Royal Aces, your strategy must pivot. In many situations where you would normally keep a "low" pair or a high-card kicker, Royal Aces dictates that you should prioritize the "Ace-chase."

1.  **Breaking Pairs:** In some edge cases, holding a lone Ace over a low pair (2s-10s) becomes the correct move because the "Quad Ace EV" is so heavily weighted.
2.  **Kicker Dependencies:** While Royal Aces doesn't typically have a "kicker" bonus (like Double Double Bonus), the 2,000-coin payout creates a psychological kicker effect. Every time you draw to Aces, you are hunting that 400:1 return.
3.  **Bankroll Management:** Because of the 2,000-coin bonus, Royal Aces has a higher variance than Bonus Poker. You need a larger bankroll to weather the storms.

## Conclusion

Understanding the **royal aces video poker strategy** starts with recognizing that you are playing for the Quads. The 9/6 table is the only one that truly offers a competitive return, whereas the 7/5 version turns the game into a high-risk lottery. Always check the pay table before you sit down—that 2,000-coin lure is only profitable if the rest of the table isn't bleeding you dry.
